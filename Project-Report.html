Motivation

Provide an overview of the project goals and motivation. As we enter the influenza season this Winter while continuing to fight against the COVID-19 pandemic, we have seen a continued urgency from public health and government officials alike, to encourage New Yorkers to get vaccinated for both influenza and COVID-19. This year in particular, experts are concerned that this year’s flu season may be even more severe, due to an unusually mild season last year (Spantideas et al, 2021). Now, with the rise of a new COVID variant, city and public health leaders are pushing for mandatory vaccination requirements and continued COVID-19 precautions to prepare for a potential “Twindemic,” or the simultaneous outbreak of influenza and COVID-19. A Twindemic has the potential to crowd emergency rooms, threaten the public health of our communities, and burden our healthcare system. Therefore, for this project, we seek to conduct an exploratory analysis between COVID-19 and influenza in New York City. Specifically, we will examine the relationship between COVID-19 and influenza uptake by zip code and sociodemographic factors in New York City through data visualizations and regression analyses.

NYC Health COVID-19

The NYC Department of Health and Mental Hygiene (NYC DOH) classifies the start of the COVID-19 outbreak in NYC by its first lab-confirmed case on February 29, 2020. Cases of COVID-19 included in their datasets are considered “confirmed” cases, or cases with a positive result from a molecular test. NYC DOH data on COVID-19 vaccines come from the Citywide Immunization Registry (CIR), which tracks immunizations for all NYC residents. A person is considered fully vaccinated if they have received both doses of the Moderna or Pfizer vaccine series, or received one dose of the Johnson & Johnson/Janssen vaccine series. There is currently no data on COVID-19 boosters. The NYC DOH COVID-19 data can be found here, and the COVID-19 vaccination data can be found here.

Exploratory Analysis

During our exploratory analysis, we first examined COVID-19 and influenza case and vaccine trends separately to better understand the data we were working with. COVID-19

Add plot image (Weekly Case Rate by Borough)

This plot shows the weekly case rate by NYC borough, beginning the week ending on August 8, 2020. From the plot, we can see that each borough followed a similar trend in cases over time. Cases peaked for all boroughs around January through February of 2021, and then another smaller peak in cases at April 2021. Cases were significantly lower after April 2021, around the time when most NYC adults were eligible to get vaccinated.

Add plot (Percent adults partially vaccinated by borough)

These plots show the percent of adults partially and fully vaccinated by borough, as of December 2021. The partially vaccinated plot excluded outliers greater than 30% and the fully vaccinated plot excluded outliers greater than 100%, as they were not representative of the dataset and may have been included erroneously. The median full-vaccination rate is greater than 75% for each borough, with Queens, Manhattan, and Staten Island having the highest full-vaccination rates, respectively. Interestingly, while Staten Island has one of the higher vaccination rates, it is consistently the borough with the highest case rate out of all the boroughs. When we examine the partially and fully vaccinated rates cumulatively, each borough is at least 80% vaccinated.

Add Plot (Distribution of Locations Providing Seasonal Flu Vaccinations)

The above bar graph examines the relationship between locations providing the seasonal flu vaccine and boroughs of NYC. Based on the graph, we can see that Manhattan has by far the largest number of locations providing the flu vaccine, followed by Brooklyn, Queens, the Bronx, and Staten Island, respectively.
